2 June 1609

King James I granted new charter to the Virginia Company of London, expanding its jurisdiction along the coast to 200 miles north and south of Point Comfort (c. 37 degrees north latitude), including islands within 100 miles, and extending westward and northwestward to the Pacific Ocean. (Paullin, pl. 42; Swindler, 10:24-36; Van Zandt, 92)

12 March 1611/1612

King James I granted new charter to the Virginia Company of London, expanding its jurisdiction to encompass all land between 30 degrees and 41 degrees north latitude, plus offshore islands within 300 leagues, including Bermuda, and by implication still extending to the Pacific Ocean. (Swindler, 10:37-45; Van Zandt, 92-93)

30 June 1665

King Charles II granted a new charter to the proprietors of Carolina, expanding its jurisdiction north and south. New boundaries were: on the north, a line from the north end of Currituck R. westward to "Wyonoak" Creek and thence due west along the parallel of 36 degrees, 30 minutes north latitude; on the west, the Pacific Ocean; and on the south, the parallel of 29 degrees north latitude. The new northern line implicitly redefined the southern limit of Virginia (including present Kentucky) as the parallel of 36 degrees, 30 minutes north latitude, and, although this definition has not changed to the present, attempts at demarcation have produced an unusually irregular boundary. (Swindler, 7:375)

1 January 1734/1735

ORANGE (Va.) created from SPOTSYLVANIA (Va.); ORANGE extended westward to "the utmost limits of Virginia," including all of Kentucky. (Hening, 4:450-451)

15 December 1738

AUGUSTA (Va.) created from ORANGE (Va.); not fully organized, attached to ORANGE. AUGUSTA included all of Kentucky; ORANGE jurisdiction over Kentucky continued indirectly through attachment. (Hening, 5:78-80)

1745

1 May 1754

AUGUSTA (Va.) lost to FREDERICK (Va.), and lost to creation of HAMPSHIRE (W. Va.). (Hening, 6:376-379)

10 February 1763

The Treaty of Paris, ending the Seven Years' War between Great Britain (the victor) and France and Spain, formally transferred Florida to the British and implicitly set the Mississippi R. as the new western limit for British colonies, including Connecticut, Georgia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Virginia (including present Kentucky), whose charter bounds had technically extended to the Pacific Ocean. (Cappon, Petchenik, and Long, 1)

31 January 1770

BOTETOURT (Va.) created from AUGUSTA (Va.). BOTETOURT included all of Kentucky; AUGUSTA eliminated from present Kentucky. (Hening, 8:395-398)

1 December 1772

FINCASTLE (Va.) created from BOTETOURT (Va.). FINCASTLE included all of Kentucky; BOTETOURT eliminated from present Kentucky. (Hening, 8:600-601)

31 December 1776

1 November 1780

FAYETTE, JEFFERSON, and LINCOLN created by Virginia from KENTUCKY County (Va.); KENTUCKY County eliminated. (Hening, 10:315-317)

1779-1780

[1779-1780] Commissioners from North Carolina and Virginia demarcated their boundary from Cumberland Mt. Westward to the Tennessee R., which became part of the modern line between Kentucky and Tennessee. This boundary line is irregular and runs from 5.75 to 12 miles north of the parallel of 36 degrees, 30 minutes north latitude, as prescribed in 1665; that irregularity apparently was the result of accommodating local property owners near the line. (Van Zandt, 110)

1 August 1782

Kentucky District (Va.), a special judicial district, was created to serve Virginians living west of the Allegheny Mountains. Its extent was described only in terms of the counties it encompassed: FAYETTE, JEFFERSON, and LINCOLN. This district became the state of Kentucky in 1792. (Hening, 11:85)

3 September 1783

Commissioners from Great Britain and the United States signed the Treaty of Paris (ratifications exchanged 12 May 1784), ending the War of the American Revolution, recognizing American independence, and defining U.S. boundaries. The new national limits were the St. Croix River-Atlantic watershed-45th parallel-St. Lawrence River-Great Lakes-Lake of the Woods line on the north; the Mississippi R. on the west; and the Floridas on the south. (Parry, 48:481, 487, 491-492; Van Zandt, 12)

1 March 1784

Virginia ceded to the United States its charter claims to territory "northwestward of the river Ohio," thereby setting the north bank of the Ohio River as the northern and northwestern limit for present Kentucky and West Virginia, a boundary that has remained unchanged to the present. (Hening, 11:571-575)
